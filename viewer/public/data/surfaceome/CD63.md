**Triage first-pass reasoning** — CD63 is a four-pass tetraspanin (TM4SF) that is predominantly resident in late endosomes/lysosomes and intracellular vesicles under steady-state conditions, with well-documented surface exposure via lysosomal exocytosis (e.g., in platelets upon activation, in mast cells after degranulation, and in various cancer cell lines). Flow cytometry on non-permeabilized cells confirms a PM pool, but the dominant compartment is lysosomal/endosomal. This fits the 'lysosomal_exocytosis' contextual bucket: lysosomal exocytosis drives CD63 to the cell surface transiently upon cellular activation or secretion. There is also a constitutive partial PM pool detected by surface biotinylation and flow, so dual_localization is plausible, but the classical story is lysosomal exocytosis bringing it to the surface. Multiple antibody-based and diagnostic tools target cell-surface CD63. No stable GPI or exclusive PM localization; inner leaflet anchoring is not the mechanism. The 'yes' verdict is disfavored because the dominant steady-state localization is intracellular (lysosomal).

**Contradicting evidence**

- *Intracellular Pool* (severity Moderate): In human umbilical cord mesenchymal stem cells (hUCMSCs), CD63 is predominantly localized to multivesicular bodies (MVBs) intracellularly rather than at the cell surface. This dominant intracellular/endosomal pool suggests that surface CD63 in this cell type is a minor fraction, qualifying the surface pool as contextual rather than constitutive.
  - Likely explanation: CD63 is a well-established lysosomal/late-endosomal tetraspanin that cycles between intracellular compartments and the plasma membrane. The predominant MVB localization seen in hUCMSCs by permeabilized immunofluorescence likely reflects the large intracellular reservoir, while a smaller surface pool still exists but may require activation or specific stimuli to become the dominant localization.
- *Intracellular Pool* (severity Low): In human cancer cell lines, a major pool of CD63 is actively routed through the lysosomal degradation pathway, as demonstrated by bafilomycin A1 rescuing CD63 protein levels after ANXA2 loss. This lysosome-dependent turnover is consistent with CD63 being predominantly intracellular/lysosomal rather than stably surface-resident.
  - Likely explanation: Lysosomal degradation of CD63 reflects its normal trafficking itinerary as a tetraspanin that shuttles between the plasma membrane and late endosomes/lysosomes. The degradation data indicate the size of the intracellular pool under specific regulatory conditions (ANXA2 loss) rather than a direct refutation of surface presence; constitutive surface CD63 is still well-documented across many cell types.
