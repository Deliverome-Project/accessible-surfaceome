## 1. Executive summary

**Surface-accessible only in disease states (complement activation, viral infection, tauopathy, thromboinflammation, tumor), where activated C3b/iC3b fragments deposit covalently onto target cell surfaces; the dominant C3 pool is freely circulating/secreted and not surface-anchored in normal resting tissue.**

C3 is state-dependently surface-accessible as a secreted complement glycoprotein — no transmembrane domain, with surface presence achieved exclusively via thioester-mediated covalent deposition of activated C3b/iC3b fragments onto target cell surfaces. Direct surface evidence is single-method: live-cell flow cytometry detecting C3 deposition on infected cells (a1_evi_23) and a functional assay showing CR2-fH reducing C3b deposits on RPE cell membranes (a1_evi_42); IHC in human tauopathy brain shows neuronal C3b/iC3b deposition (a1_evi_22). Surface presence is strictly state-gated, requiring complement activation triggers — viral infection, thromboinflammation, oncogenic transformation, or neurodegenerative disease — and is absent in normal resting tissue (a2_evi_12, a2_evi_13). A dominant free-soluble circulating pool is the principal antibody-decoy risk (a1_evi_01, a1_evi_03); moderate conformational epitope masking arises from activation-state-dependent neoepitope exposure (a1_evi_17, a1_evi_18).

**Family / classification** — HGNC gene group(s): Receptor ligands; C3 and PZP like, alpha-2-macroglobulin domain containing; Complement system activation components · functional class: Miscellaneous.

**Triage first-pass reasoning** — C3 is a secreted plasma protein synthesized primarily by the liver and macrophages. It circulates in blood as a soluble alpha-beta disulfide-linked heterodimer (~185 kDa). It has no transmembrane domain, no GPI anchor, and no known stable membrane partner that would co-traffic it to the cell surface. Checking each contextual bucket: (1) cell_state_induced — C3 deposits covalently on opsonized targets via its thioester-mediated attachment, but this is matrix/surface deposition on pathogens/debris, not PM display of the C3 protein body accessible from outside a host cell. (2) tissue_restricted_surface — no evidence of lineage-specific PM display. (3) lysosomal_exocytosis — not documented. (4) dual_localization — no evidence of a stable PM pool; covalent deposition on cells is on target cells/pathogens, not on C3-secreting cells. (5) stable_surface_attachment — opsonization deposits C3b/iC3b on target surfaces, but this reflects extracellular covalent attachment to glycoproteins/carbohydrates on those target cells, not the C3-producing cell's own PM. No antibody/ADC/CAR-T programs target C3 on a host cell surface. Anti-C3 therapeutics (e.g., pegcetacoplan) neutralize the soluble circulating form. Verdict: secreted_only.

**Contradicting evidence**

- *Intracellular Pool* (severity High): C3 is a secreted glycoprotein with no transmembrane domain, present both in circulation and intracellularly in hepatocytes. A significant intracellular pool of C3 in hepatocytes serves functions in autophagy, ER function, and lipid homeostasis, consistent with a secreted/intracellular rather than plasma-membrane-anchored classification.
  - Likely explanation: C3 lacks a transmembrane domain and is a classical secreted complement protein. Intracellular C3 in hepatocytes represents a biosynthetic/pre-secretory pool or a distinct intracellular complement system, not surface-anchored protein. Any apparent surface detection likely reflects covalent deposition of activated C3 fragments (C3b, iC3b) on cell surfaces rather than constitutive transmembrane expression.
- *Secreted Only* (severity Moderate): In iPSC-derived retinal pigment epithelium cells, C3 and its fragments (C3a, C3(H2O), iC3b) are predominantly secreted apically and basally in mature cells, while less mature cells retain C3 fragments intracellularly. This partitioning between secretion and intracellular retention demonstrates that C3 is not a stable plasma-membrane protein in RPE.
  - Likely explanation: RPE cells are known to secrete complement components as part of their homeostatic function. Intracellular retention in less mature cells likely reflects incomplete processing through the secretory pathway rather than a distinct surface-expression compartment. The dominant fate of C3 in RPE is secretion, not membrane anchoring.
- *Intracellular Pool* (severity Moderate): Intracellular complement activation in spleen stromal fibroblastic reticular cells (FRCs) generates C3a via the alternative pathway following SFTSV infection, demonstrating that C3 can be activated entirely within the intracellular compartment in non-hepatic cell types, further reinforcing that C3 biology is not restricted to the plasma membrane surface.
  - Likely explanation: Intracellular complement activation is a recognized parallel system to classical extracellular complement. C3 synthesized within FRCs undergoes intracellular processing without obligate surface trafficking. This does not preclude transient surface deposition of activated fragments, but underscores that C3 is not a bona fide surface-resident protein.
- *Other* (severity Low): ECM components (brevican, aggrecan) in mouse cerebellum can shield synaptic surfaces from C3b tagging, and protein corona components on nanoparticles can mask HC-bound C3 fragments from complement receptor recognition. These epitope-masking phenomena indicate that even when C3 fragments are deposited on surfaces, their accessibility to receptors can be substantially reduced by surrounding molecular environments.
  - Likely explanation: These masking effects are context-dependent and do not negate C3 surface deposition per se; rather, they modulate functional accessibility of deposited C3 fragments. For target-discovery purposes, the relevant question is whether C3 fragments are present on cell surfaces, which these findings do not directly contradict — they only show that detection or receptor engagement can be hindered in specific microenvironments.
