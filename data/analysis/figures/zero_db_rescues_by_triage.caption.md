**Figure 3. The triage agent rescues 960 surface candidates that no source database flags.** Of the 19,324 protein-coding genes in the whole-proteome catalog, 13,698 (71%) sit in the zero-database slice where none of the five reference sources (UniProt, GO cellular component, the Human Protein Atlas, SURFY, and CSPA) marks the protein as surface-localized — here "no database flags it" means under the bench-optimized cutoffs, the same thresholds the accuracy figures use to score each database. Within that slice the two-stage Sonnet 4.6 triage agent calls 81 proteins definitively surface ("yes", panel a) and 879 conditionally surface ("contextual": state- or lineage-dependent, panel b). The agent first reviews each gene using NCBI gene and protein records; genes it initially rejects for an ambiguous reason are re-examined with a literature-search pass, which upgraded 166 of the 960 rescues (the remaining 794 came from the first pass). Bars group each verdict by the agent's stated rationale — classical receptors, multipass channels with exposed loops, GPI-anchored and stable-complex partners on the definite side; dual-localized, tissue-restricted, surface-tethered, cell-state-induced, and secretion-exposed proteins on the contextual side. Representative examples are listed per rationale — LVRN, STEAP3, NYX, and LY96 on the definite side; IL15, KLK2, MMP9, LRG1, GSDME, HSPA1A, and HPSE on the contextual side (two clinically-pursued exemplars are shown for the surface-tethered and cell-state-induced classes) — each belonging to the reason category shown above it. Every one is independently documented in the primary literature as reaching the cell surface (by flow cytometry, surface biotinylation, or an equivalent assay), yet none is flagged by any of the five databases even under the optimized cutoffs — illustrating the kind of real, actionable surface biology the gating databases miss.
