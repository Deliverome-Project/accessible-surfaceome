Executive summary

SRC is canonically an intracellular, N-myristoylated kinase, but in cancer cells it undergoes topological inversion via autophagolysosomal exocytosis (ALE), exposing its kinase domain on the extracellular face of the plasma membrane as eSrc. This cancer-selective surface form is directly supported by live-cell functional assays, surface biotinylation (U251 MG; WB+MS dual readout), and in vivo xenograft antibody-killing studies. Surface accessibility is thus high in cancer but essentially absent in normal cells, making state dependence high and creating a favorable cancer-selective targeting window.

Confidence reasoning

moderate A1 grades evidence as direct_multi_method (live-cell functional assays + surface biotinylation + xenograft antibody engagement from independent sources, no contradicting evidence), which normally supports high confidence. Confidence is held at moderate because: (1) state dependence is high — eSrc surface form is exclusive to cancer cells via ALE, absent in normal cells, so the surface-accessible form is disease-state-conditional; (2) the ECD presented is the inverted kinase domain with minimal characterized extracellular surface, introducing uncertainty about binder accessibility; (3) A2 confirms junctional restriction in normal tissues as a background concern. The evidence base for the cancer-state surface form is strong, but the narrow biological window and non-canonical topology introduce meaningful translational uncertainty.

surface-form rationale
SRC is canonically an intracellular, N-myristoylated kinase. In cancer cells it undergoes topological inversion via autophagolysosomal exocytosis (ALE), exposing its kinase domain on the extracellular face of the plasma membrane. Anti-Src antibody therapeutics engage this surface-inverted, membrane-anchored form (eSrc). No secreted soluble form of Src is documented in this ledger.
