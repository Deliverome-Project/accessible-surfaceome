## Figure 4 — `deep_dive_flow`

- **Gist:** [gist](https://gist.github.com/beccajcarlson/eb7ce1015ff21ec0329472e33a9ea713) · `swh:1:rev:506d29ab3052576e6db9b33782e55b5d975483de`
- **Bundled TSV:** _(SVG mockup — no data TSV)_

**Figure 4. The per-gene deep-dive pipeline runs in three sequential stages, producing one validated record.** Triage-candidate genes flow top to bottom; box color encodes the engine performing each step. Stage 1, evidence retrieval (deterministic, no LLM): the union of NCBI gene2pubmed, Europe PMC, and PubTator builds a candidate corpus (median 234.5 papers/gene), a per-paper Haiku 4.5 abstract triage selects papers worth fetching, and full-text recovery is attempted through PMC JATS, then an Unpaywall open-access PDF, then a DataCite landing-page scrape — each tier falling through on a miss — yielding a trimmed evidence ledger via a Sonnet 4.6 selector. Stage 2, block builders (Sonnet 4.6): a concurrent fan-out emits surface-evidence blocks (detection methods, contradictions) and surface-biology blocks (expression, localization, anatomical accessibility, accessibility modulation); an evidence-grade block is graded against the methods, and a cross-focus risks block reads merged evidence plus deterministic features to score shedding, secreted forms, ectodomain size, and epitope masking — nine in all. Stage 3, synthesizer (Sonnet 4.6): reads every block draft, the Stage 1 ledgers, the deterministic-features payload, and the triage prior, then emits the executive summary, structured filters, and confidence rationale. Each record is validated against the record specification and published to disk, the viewer fallback, and the public catalog, at roughly $2–3 per gene.

**The deep-dive agent sorts the roughly 5,000 triage-positive candidates into five biology-driven surface-call buckets, with cell-state-induced presentation broken out by what triggers it.** This figure is a MOCK rendered against placeholder counts (cohort n = 5,000) and will be re-rendered once the deep-dive sweep finishes; the bucket definitions and the trigger taxonomy shown here are final. Each gene falls into one bucket. The "canonical surface" bucket collects reasons that mark a protein as a constitutive surface resident — classical receptors, membrane-spanning proteins with exposed loops, lipid-anchored proteins, extracellular-facing proteins, and stable surface-complex partners. ## Supp S5 — `ensemble_vs_best_db_vs_sonnet`

**Supplementary Figure 5. Ensembling the five gold-standard surface databases does not close the accuracy gap to the Sonnet 4.6 triage agent.** Overall accuracy on the 147-gene SurfaceBench (soft-credit rule) is shown for six callers: the production triage agent (Sonnet 4.6 with gene identifiers; bar height is the mean of three replicates, overlaid as points with a SEM error bar), the single best-performing database (UniProt, calling a gene surface on a strict surface term, an extracellular topology assignment, a transmembrane segment, or a signal peptide), and four ensemble callers requiring at least two, three, four, or all five databases to agree. Combining the databases therefore suppresses surface calls faster than it corrects them and never recovers the proteins they jointly miss.

## Supp S10 — `paywall_bot_block_compare`

- **Gist:** [gist](https://gist.github.com/beccajcarlson/cbc950dad1c3a6595fd5018cdb6b030d) · `swh:1:rev:8ab41cc6ef790b01df229633fd35a6ef2a19e958`
- **Bundled TSV:** `paywall_bot_block_compare.tsv` (1,989 rows · 504.4 KB)

**Casting a wider literature net surfaces several times more papers per gene, but most of the additional papers are unreachable, which is why the broader strategy was not adopted.** To pick a discovery strategy for cohort-scale runs, two were compared on the same 100 randomly sampled genes, 10 random papers per gene each. The production strategy searches EuropePMC and adds machine-annotated mentions from PubTator, NCBI's curated gene-to-publication links, and a few standing topic queries; the alternative issues one broad gene-name query plus nine method-keyword queries against the OpenAlex index, which also reaches preprints and non-PubMed journals. Each sampled paper was run through the production retrieval chain — full text from PubMed Central, then an open-access PDF located through Unpaywall, then a publisher PDF reached via a paper's machine-readable full-text link — and binned by where retrieval succeeded or why it failed. The two strategies discovered very different volumes: a median of 234.5 candidate papers per gene for production versus 858.5 for OpenAlex. But reachability collapsed on the larger pool. Production recovered full text for about 89% of its papers (88% from PubMed Central), with only 10% wholly paywalled; OpenAlex reached just 48% (44% from PubMed Central), with 38% paywalled and a further 11% nominally open but served only by publishers that block automated access. Unpaywall rescued 3.8% of OpenAlex papers, against 0.5% for production. The extra volume thus added mostly inaccessible literature at proportionally higher cost, so production discovery was kept.
