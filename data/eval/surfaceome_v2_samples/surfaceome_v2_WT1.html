Executive summary

WT1 is a nuclear/cytoplasmic transcription factor with no credible direct surface protein accessibility. All surface-engagement evidence in the ledger concerns processed WT1 peptide fragments presented via MHC-I (pMHC) at extremely low density (<100 sites/cell by flow cytometry), not the WT1 protein itself. A2 confirms exclusively nuclear/cytoplasmic localization across podocytes, mesothelial cells, and CAFs—all detected only with permeabilization. There is no transmembrane domain, no shed or secreted form, and no surface subdomain evidence. Therapeutic strategies target the pMHC complex, not WT1 protein directly.

surface-form rationale
WT1 is a nuclear transcription factor with no transmembrane or secreted isoform documented in this ledger. Therapeutics engage it exclusively via processed WT1 peptide fragments presented by MHC-I (HLA-A2) at the tumor cell surface — a pMHC complex, not the WT1 protein itself. Contradicting evidence (1)

other severity: moderate
Flow cytometry on live tumor cells failed to detect WT1-derived peptide/MHC-I complexes at the cell surface, with endogenous antigen density estimated to be below ~100 sites per cell — the sensitivity floor of flow cytometry. This indicates that physiological WT1 surface presentation is too sparse to be measured by standard flow-based methods, raising a meaningful caveat for claims of surface accessibility.
likely explanation
WT1 is a nuclear transcription factor expressed at low levels; the peptide/MHC-I complexes it generates at the cell surface reflect normal antigen-processing turnover and are likely present but at sub-detection densities (<100 copies/cell). This is a sensitivity limitation of flow cytometry rather than proof of complete absence — TCR-based or mass-spectrometry immunopeptidomics approaches can detect such low-density targets — but it does temper enthusiasm for antibody- or ADC-based surface targeting strategies that require higher epitope copy numbers.
