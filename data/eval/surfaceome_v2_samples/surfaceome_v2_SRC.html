Executive summary

SRC is canonically an intracellular N-myristoylated kinase, but in cancer cells it undergoes topological inversion onto the outer plasma membrane leaflet (eSrc) via autophagolysosomal exocytosis (ALE). eSrc is detected in primary human tumors and xenografts; anti-Src antibodies mediate tumor cell killing in vitro and in vivo. Surface accessibility is therefore high but strictly cancer-state-dependent — normal cells retain SRC intracellularly. Under normal conditions SRC shows junctional/vesicular localization with limited systemic reachability.

Section 1 — Surface evidence

evidence grade
direct_single_method
grade rationale
All direct surface-accessibility evidence originates from a single source (PMID:41818370) and its companion paper (PMID:41818382), both reporting the same discovery that Src undergoes topological inversion onto the cancer cell surface via autophagolysosomal exocytosis (ALE). The functional assays (antibody-mediated tumor killing in xenografts, eSrc detection in primary tumors) and the companion abstract corroborate surface exposure, but they rely on a single method class—live-cell antibody engagement/functional killing assays—and are not truly independent methodological approaches (e.g., no separate surface biotinylation or flow cytometry panel). No meaningful contradiction undermines the surface claim; the IHC simply reflects the canonical localization. Grade: direct_single_method.

Therapeutic engagement

stage: preclinical_in_vivo
description
Anti-Src antibody-based therapeutics targeting the extracellular face of eSrc (extracellular membrane-associated Src) have been demonstrated to mediate tumor cell killing in cell culture systems and in mouse xenograft models (PMID:41818370). eSrc was also detected in primary human tumors. The program is preclinical; no named sponsor or clinical trial is documented in this ledger. The therapeutic rationale is based on cancer-specific topological inversion of Src onto the outer plasma membrane surface, enabling antibody access to an otherwise intracellular kinase.
surface-form rationale
Src is canonically an intracellular N-myristoylated protein. In cancer, it undergoes topological inversion via autophagolysosomal exocytosis (ALE), exposing it at the outer plasma membrane as eSrc. Anti-Src antibody therapeutics engage this cancer-specific surface-exposed form. Standard IHC scores SRC as cytoplasmic (a1_evi_06), underscoring that the surface form is a cancer-specific, non-secreted membrane-anchored topology rather than a constitutive or secreted isoform.
